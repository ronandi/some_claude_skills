# Anthropic Values Research

Research compiled from Anthropic's publicly available materials: job postings, blog posts, published papers, interviews with leadership, Claude's Character document, and the Responsible Scaling Policy. This document captures the values signals that inform their behavioral interview round.

---

## Anthropic's Core Values (Public Signals)

### From Job Postings (Recurring Language)

Anthropic job postings consistently use these phrases, which reveal what they screen for:

- **"Collaborative, low-ego environment"** -- They explicitly screen against lone-wolf mentality. Stories should feature genuine collaboration.
- **"Comfort with ambiguity"** -- Repeated across nearly every posting. This is not a throwaway line; it is a filter.
- **"Intellectual honesty"** -- Valuing truth over being right. They want people who update their beliefs.
- **"Mission-driven"** -- AI safety is not a nice-to-have for culture fit; it is load-bearing.
- **"Empirical mindset"** -- They value evidence over intuition, measurement over conviction.

### From Blog Posts and Research Papers

- **Responsible Scaling Policy**: Demonstrates a commitment to operating under self-imposed constraints even when economically costly. Values candidates who understand the concept of voluntary limitation.
- **Constitutional AI**: The idea that values can be encoded systematically, not just felt. Values candidates who can reason about values explicitly, not just "I know it when I see it."
- **Interpretability Research**: Deep interest in understanding why systems behave as they do. Values candidates who ask "why" not just "what."
- **Claude's Character document**: Published publicly, emphasizing honesty, harmlessness, and helpfulness -- in that priority order. The ordering matters.

### From Leadership Interviews (Dario Amodei, Daniela Amodei, Chris Olah, others)

Key themes that emerge from public interviews:

1. **The race dynamics are real**: Anthropic acknowledges competitive pressure while arguing for restraint. Values candidates who can hold this tension without dismissing either side.
2. **Uncertainty is the honest position**: On many AI safety questions, "we don't know" is more honest than confident predictions in either direction. Values candidates comfortable saying this.
3. **The goal is beneficial AI, not no AI**: Anthropic is not anti-AI-development. They believe in building powerful AI safely. Candidates who position as pure doomers or pure accelerationists both miss the mark.
4. **Small teams, high trust**: Anthropic operates with smaller teams than comparably-valued companies. Trust and judgment matter more than process.

## What Anthropic Looks For vs. Screens Against

### What They Look For

| Signal | How It Manifests in Behavioral Answers |
|--------|---------------------------------------|
| Intellectual humility | "I was wrong about X, and here's specifically when I realized it" |
| Nuanced ethical reasoning | Holds complexity; doesn't collapse gray areas to black/white |
| Genuine mission alignment | Can articulate specific AI safety concerns with technical depth |
| Collaborative instinct | Credits others, describes collective problem-solving |
| Comfort with uncertainty | Can describe operating effectively without clear answers |
| Self-awareness | Knows their own patterns, biases, and blind spots |
| Empirical mindset | Relies on evidence, changes views when data contradicts belief |
| Constructive disagreement | Can disagree clearly without damaging relationships |
| Long-term thinking | Weighs second and third-order consequences |
| Curiosity | Asks questions beyond what's required; explores adjacent domains |

### What They Screen Against

| Red Flag | How It Manifests |
|----------|-----------------|
| Ego-driven narratives | Every story features them as the smartest person in the room |
| Dismissal of safety concerns | "AI risk is overblown" or treating safety as a PR exercise |
| Rule-following without reasoning | "I did it because the policy said to" without engaging with why |
| Binary thinking | Every situation has a clear right answer; no tolerance for ambiguity |
| Competitive framing | "I won" language; zero-sum thinking about colleagues |
| Surface-level motivation | "I want to work here because you're a top AI company" |
| Rehearsed inauthenticity | Perfect stories with no genuine hesitation or complexity |
| Credit hoarding | Cannot name a specific peer's contribution |
| Defensiveness under follow-up | Redirects, deflects, or gets tense when probed deeper |
| Certainty about the unknowable | Confident predictions about AI timelines, capabilities, or risks without hedging |

## How Anthropic's Behavioral Round Differs from FAANG

### Structural Differences

**FAANG behavioral rounds** typically:
- Map answers to a published leadership principles framework (Amazon LPs, Google Googleyness, Meta core values)
- Follow a predictable 2-3 question format per round
- Evaluate against a rubric tied to specific principles
- Allow "strong" answers that clearly demonstrate the target principle
- Rarely go deeper than 3 follow-up levels

**Anthropic behavioral rounds** typically:
- Do not map to a published framework (no "Anthropic Leadership Principles" checklist)
- May spend an entire 45-minute round on 2-3 questions, going very deep
- Evaluate holistically -- the interviewer forms an impression of character, not a score
- Value answers that demonstrate complexity and self-awareness over clear "wins"
- Routinely go 5-6 follow-up levels deep on a single story
- Deliberately use negative framing to test comfort with vulnerability

### Framing Differences

| FAANG Style | Anthropic Style |
|------------|-----------------|
| "Tell me about a time you demonstrated leadership" | "Tell me about a time you were wrong" |
| "How did you influence a team?" | "When did you fail to influence and what happened?" |
| "Describe a time you delivered results under pressure" | "What's a project that failed? What was your role in the failure?" |
| "How do you handle ambiguity?" | "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision where there was no right answer" |
| "Why do you want to work here?" | "What concerns you about AI development, and what do you think you can personally do about it?" |

### Evaluation Differences

**At FAANG**: The interviewer evaluates "did they demonstrate this LP at the target level?"

**At Anthropic**: The interviewer evaluates:
- "Do I trust this person's judgment?"
- "Would I want to work through a hard problem with them?"
- "Are they honest with themselves?"
- "Do they care about the mission in a way that will sustain through hard days?"
- "Can they hold complexity without simplifying prematurely?"

## AI Safety Mission Alignment: Going Deep

The "why AI safety?" question is not a formality. Interviewers have heard every surface-level answer. To stand out, your response must demonstrate:

### Depth Signals

1. **Technical understanding**: You understand, at least at a high level, why alignment is hard. Not just "AI could be dangerous" but specific challenges (reward hacking, goal misgeneralization, deceptive alignment, mesa-optimization, scalable oversight).

2. **Personal connection**: There is a specific moment, project, or experience that made this personal for you. Not "I read about AI risk" but "I built X and saw Y and it changed how I think about Z."

3. **Epistemic humility**: You acknowledge uncertainty about timelines and specific risks. "I think the most important thing is that we don't know" is stronger than confident doom or confident optimism.

4. **Active engagement**: You have done something concrete -- read specific papers, contributed to open-source safety tools, changed a project's direction, joined relevant communities, or built with safety considerations.

5. **Sustained interest**: This is not a recent pivot driven by job market conditions. You can point to a trajectory of increasing engagement over months or years.

### What NOT to Say

- "I want to work on the most important problem of our time" (too generic, sounds like every applicant)
- "I think AI could destroy humanity and we need to stop it" (Anthropic builds AI; this is misaligned with their mission)
- "AI safety seems like an interesting research area" (too academic, not mission-driven)
- "I want to work at a top AI lab" (competence-seeking, not mission-seeking)
- "I've been following AI safety since ChatGPT came out" (too recent, too trend-driven)

### What TO Say (Directional, Not Scripts)

- Connect to a specific technical experience that revealed the difficulty of alignment
- Reference specific papers, concepts, or debates in the field
- Acknowledge the tension between capability and safety and where you personally sit
- Describe a concrete action you took that was informed by safety concerns
- Be honest about what you do not understand and want to learn

## Values-Specific Question Patterns

Based on publicly available interview experience reports and Anthropic's stated values:

### The Intellectual Honesty Probe

**Setup**: "Tell me about a time your team was working under a wrong assumption."
**What they dig for**: Did you notice? Did you speak up? What happened when you did? If you did not speak up, why not?
**Deep follow-up**: "What would have happened if you'd spoken up earlier? Why didn't you?"

### The Collaborative Rigor Probe

**Setup**: "Describe a technical disagreement where the other person was right."
**What they dig for**: Can you name the person? Can you articulate their argument better than your own? Do you credit them fully?
**Deep follow-up**: "What was it about their reasoning that you initially missed? What does that tell you about your blind spots?"

### The Ethical Reasoning Probe

**Setup**: "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision where the right thing to do wasn't clear."
**What they dig for**: Can you hold moral complexity? Do you have a framework or do you wing it? Did you consult others?
**Deep follow-up**: "Would you make the same decision today? What if the consequences had been different?"

### The Comfort with Uncertainty Probe

**Setup**: "What's a big open question in your field that you think about a lot?"
**What they dig for**: Can you articulate genuine uncertainty? Do you sit with not-knowing or rush to answers?
**Deep follow-up**: "What would change your mind on this? What evidence would you need?"

### The Self-Awareness Probe

**Setup**: "What feedback have you received that was hard to hear but turned out to be right?"
**What they dig for**: Can you receive critical feedback without defensiveness? Did you actually change?
**Deep follow-up**: "How long did it take you to accept it? What was the gap between hearing it and believing it?"

## Preparation Checklist for Anthropic Values Round

- [ ] Can articulate why AI safety matters in your own words (not parroting company messaging)
- [ ] Have at least 3 stories where you were genuinely wrong and can describe the learning
- [ ] Can name specific peers whose ideas were better than yours
- [ ] Can describe an ethical gray area you navigated without simplifying it
- [ ] Have read at least 2-3 Anthropic blog posts and can reference specific ideas
- [ ] Can describe the difference between "building AI safely" and "not building AI"
- [ ] Have processed your failure stories enough that telling them does not trigger defensiveness
- [ ] Can answer "what don't you know?" without it sounding performative
- [ ] Have specific examples of changing your mind based on evidence
- [ ] Can discuss the competitive dynamics of AI development with nuance
