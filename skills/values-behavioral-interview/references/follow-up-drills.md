# Follow-Up Drills

Structured practice exercises for handling the deep follow-up questions that distinguish values interviews from standard behavioral rounds. Each drill targets a specific dimension of depth that interviewers probe.

**How to use these drills**: Pick a story from your story bank. Run it through one drill at a time. Record yourself (audio only). Listen back. If you sound scripted at any point, that layer needs more processing.

---

## Drill 1: The 5 Whys

**Purpose**: Build comfort with being asked "why?" at progressively deeper levels. Most candidates prepare for 2 levels of "why" and break down at level 3-4.

**Setup**: Choose any story from your bank. Have a practice partner (or record yourself) ask "why?" after every answer, 5 times in succession.

### Example Run

**Story**: "I escalated a disagreement about data privacy to the VP."

| Level | Question | Response Quality Guide |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| Why 1 | "Why did you escalate?" | **Strong**: "Because my manager's position prioritized shipping velocity over a consent gap I identified." **Weak**: "Because we disagreed." |
| Why 2 | "Why did that consent gap matter to you?" | **Strong**: "Because I'd seen how ambiguous consent language gets weaponized in FTC investigations, and I'd rather slow down than expose users." **Weak**: "Because privacy is important." |
| Why 3 | "Why are you the kind of person who notices consent gaps?" | **Strong**: "Honestly, I had a formative experience early in my career where I didn't notice, and a colleague had to flag it. That embarrassment made me hyper-attentive." **Weak**: "I've always been detail-oriented." |
| Why 4 | "Why did that early experience affect you so strongly?" | **Strong**: "Because I realized I'd been thinking about privacy as a compliance checkbox, not as a user trust issue. The gap between those framings was bigger than I expected." **Weak**: "It taught me a lesson." |
| Why 5 | "Why do you frame it as user trust rather than compliance?" | **Strong**: "Because compliance is binary and backward-looking. Trust is continuous and forward-looking. You can be compliant and still betray user expectations. That distinction now drives most of my product decisions." **Weak**: "I think trust is just more important." |

### What to Watch For

- **Breaking point**: Where does your preparation end and genuine thinking begin? That transition should feel natural, not panicked.
- **Circular answers**: Repeating the same reason in different words means you have not actually gone deeper.
- **Abstraction escape**: Jumping from concrete story to abstract principles to avoid the emotional truth.
- **"I've always been..."**: This phrase almost always signals the candidate has stopped thinking and started defending.

### Practice Protocol

1. Run 5 Whys on your 3 strongest stories
2. Run 5 Whys on your 2 weakest stories (the ones that feel most vulnerable)
3. Note where you break down -- those are the layers that need more reflection
4. Write out the honest answers to the levels where you broke down
5. Run again after 2-3 days of processing

## Drill 2: The Alternative Path

**Purpose**: Test whether you have genuinely evaluated your decision or just defended the path you took. Interviewers use this to detect post-hoc rationalization.

**Setup**: After telling a story, the interviewer proposes an alternative action and asks you to evaluate it honestly.

### Example Questions

- "What if you had gone to the VP first instead of trying to convince your manager?"
- "What if you had just let the feature ship and raised the concern afterward?"
- "What if you had done nothing -- how would things have played out?"
- "What if you had brought the junior engineer's idea to the team instead of dismissing it?"

### What Strong Answers Sound Like

**Strong**: "That's a path I considered. The advantage would have been [X]. The reason I didn't take it was [Y], but honestly, if I'm being real, the alternative might have been better because [Z]. I think I chose my path partly because [honest reason that might not be flattering]."

**Weak**: "I don't think that would have worked because [defends original decision]. My approach was better because [repeats original reasoning]."

### The Key Insight

Strong candidates can argue the alternative path's merits better than the interviewer expects. They have already thought about it. Weak candidates reflexively defend their actual decision because admitting the alternative might have been better feels like admitting failure.

### Practice Protocol

1. For each story, write down 3 alternative paths you could have taken
2. For each alternative, write an honest assessment: what would have been better and worse
3. Identify which alternative genuinely might have been the better choice
4. Practice saying "you know, that alternative might have actually been better because..." without it feeling like a concession

## Drill 3: The Critic

**Purpose**: Build comfort with direct challenges to your decisions. Interviewers sometimes play devil's advocate to see if you get defensive or engage thoughtfully.

**Setup**: After telling a story, the practice partner frames your decision as a potential mistake and watches how you respond.

### Example Challenges

- "It sounds like you prioritized being right over maintaining the relationship with your manager."
- "Couldn't you argue that escalating was actually risk-averse, not courageous?"
- "From what you described, it seems like the team suffered because of your decision."
- "That sounds like you were trying to be a hero rather than a team player."

### What Strong Answers Sound Like

**Strong**: "That's a fair read. The escalation did damage the relationship, and I've thought about whether there was a way to get the same outcome without that cost. I think the answer is maybe -- if I'd framed my concern differently in the first conversation, I might not have needed to escalate. The escalation was right on the substance but maybe not on the process."

**Weak**: "No, I had to escalate because [repeats justification]. There was no other option." (Defensive, closed to the challenge)

**Also weak**: "You're totally right, it was a mistake." (Over-capitulating without engagement. Interviewers do not want agreement; they want reasoning.)

### The Key Insight

The critic drill tests two things simultaneously:
1. Can you receive a critical framing without getting defensive?
2. Can you engage with the criticism substantively without either dismissing it or collapsing?

The sweet spot is: "That's a legitimate critique. Here's how I think about it now, which includes some of that concern."

### Practice Protocol

1. Have your practice partner read your stories and prepare the most uncomfortable critique they can construct
2. Practice responding to each critique out loud
3. Record yourself and listen for defensive tone (faster speech, higher pitch, "but" as first word)
4. Practice pausing for 3-5 seconds before responding (this alone eliminates most defensiveness)

## Drill 4: The Self-Awareness Probe

**Purpose**: Develop the ability to extract genuine meta-insights from your stories. This is the level most candidates cannot reach because it requires honest self-examination, not just story recall.

**Setup**: After telling a story, the interviewer asks what the story reveals about your patterns, tendencies, and decision-making style.

### Example Questions

- "What does this story reveal about how you handle authority?"
- "Is there a pattern in when you decide to speak up versus stay quiet?"
- "What does your approach to this situation tell you about your risk tolerance?"
- "If I heard this story from your manager's perspective, what would be different?"
- "What's the common thread between this story and the last one you told me?"

### What Strong Answers Sound Like

**Strong**: "I notice I have a pattern of being comfortable with interpersonal conflict when I believe the technical argument is clear, but I avoid conflict when the issue is more subjective or values-based. In this story, I escalated because I had data. In situations where it's a judgment call, I tend to defer. I'm working on being more willing to advocate for judgment-based positions, not just data-backed ones."

**Weak**: "I think it shows I'm someone who stands up for what's right." (Too simple, too flattering)

**Also weak**: "I don't know, I just handled it the way I thought was best." (Refuses to engage with self-examination)

### The Key Insight

Self-awareness is not self-criticism. The interviewer is not looking for you to identify flaws. They are looking for:
- **Pattern recognition**: Can you see themes across your own behavior?
- **Specificity**: Not "I'm too detail-oriented" but "I tend to over-index on technical correctness when the real issue is stakeholder alignment"
- **Agency**: Awareness that leads to intentional behavior change, not just observation

### Practice Protocol

1. Tell 3 different stories back-to-back (record them)
2. Listen to all 3 in sequence
3. Write down the patterns you notice across them
4. Ask: "What would a psychologist say about the person telling these stories?"
5. The gap between your self-image and that psychologist's assessment is where the real self-awareness lives

## Drill 5: The Values Conflict

**Purpose**: Build comfort navigating situations where two good things are in tension. This is core to Anthropic's interview philosophy -- they deal with competing goods daily (capability vs. safety, speed vs. thoroughness, user requests vs. user welfare).

**Setup**: The interviewer presents a values conflict related to your story and asks how you navigate it.

### Example Conflicts

- "What if the right technical decision would have caused two people on your team to leave?"
- "What if being honest with the client about the timeline would have lost the contract?"
- "What if the safe approach was also the one that let the competitor ship first?"
- "What if protecting user privacy meant degrading the experience for millions of users?"
- "What if your manager's approach was wrong for the company but right for the team?"

### What Strong Answers Sound Like

**Strong**: "That's the real tension. I don't think there's a clean answer. In the specific case you're describing, I'd probably lean toward [X] because [reasoning], but I want to name that choosing [X] means accepting [real cost Y]. And I'd want to make that trade-off explicitly with the team, not just decide it myself, because this is the kind of decision where having more perspectives genuinely changes the calculus."

**Weak**: "I'd always choose the ethical option." (Oversimplifies; does not acknowledge real costs)

**Also weak**: "I'd let the data decide." (Abdicates moral reasoning to metrics)

### The Key Insight

Values conflicts are not puzzles to solve. They are tensions to navigate. The interviewer is not looking for the right answer -- they are looking for:
- **Acknowledgment**: You see both sides as genuinely valid
- **Framework**: You have a way of reasoning through tensions, not just going with gut
- **Transparency**: You would name the trade-off to stakeholders, not hide it
- **Humility**: You acknowledge reasonable people could decide differently

### Practice Protocol

1. For each story in your bank, identify the values conflict inherent in the situation
2. Write out the strongest version of the argument against your actual decision
3. Practice saying: "The strongest argument against what I did is..." without it feeling like a concession
4. Have your practice partner escalate the conflict: "But what if the cost was even higher? What's your breaking point?"
5. Find your actual breaking point -- where would you have decided differently? Knowing this shows genuine engagement with the tradeoff.

## Drill Integration: The Full Gauntlet

After practicing each drill individually, combine them in a simulated 30-minute round.

### Gauntlet Structure

1. **Opening** (5 min): Tell your story. Practice partner listens without interrupting.
2. **5 Whys** (5 min): Drill deeper into the reasoning chain.
3. **Alternative Path** (5 min): "What if you had done X instead?"
4. **Critic** (5 min): "It sounds like that decision might have been..."
5. **Self-Awareness** (5 min): "What pattern do you see in how you handled this?"
6. **Values Conflict** (5 min): "What if [competing good] had been at stake?"

### Evaluation Criteria

After the gauntlet, assess yourself on:

| Dimension | 1 (Weak) | 3 (Adequate) | 5 (Strong) |
|-----------|----------|-------------|------------|
| Depth | Stayed at surface level | Reached tradeoff/meta-reflection | Reached worldview level naturally |
| Defensiveness | Got visibly defensive | Stayed neutral | Engaged openly with challenges |
| Authenticity | Sounded rehearsed throughout | Genuine in later levels | Genuine throughout, natural variation |
| Self-awareness | Could not identify patterns | Named patterns when asked | Volunteered patterns unprompted |
| Complexity | Binary answers | Some nuance | Held multiple truths simultaneously |
| Specificity | Generic lessons learned | Named specific changes | Connected to current behavior |

### Passing Score

- Average 3+ across all dimensions: Ready for the round
- Any dimension at 1: That story needs more processing before interview day
- All dimensions at 4+: You are well-prepared; focus on staying natural rather than preparing more
