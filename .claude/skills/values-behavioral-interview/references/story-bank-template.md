# Story Bank Template

Build a bank of 8-12 career stories that cover the full spread of values interview question categories. Each story should be deeply prepared through all 6 levels of the Follow-Up Ladder and adaptable to multiple question framings.

---

## Story Entry Template

Copy this template for each story in your bank.

### Story [#]: [Working Title]

**Metadata**
- **Time period**: [Month/Year - Month/Year]
- **Company/Project**: [Where this happened]
- **Your role**: [Title and scope of responsibility]
- **Category tags**: [Check all that apply]
  - [ ] Failure & Learning
  - [ ] Conflict & Disagreement
  - [ ] Mission & Motivation
  - [ ] Self-Awareness & Growth
  - [ ] Ethics & Trade-offs
  - [ ] Ambiguity & Uncertainty
- **Primary question it answers**: [The question this story is strongest for]
- **Secondary questions it adapts to**: [2-3 other question types]

---

**STAR-L Structure**

**Situation** (2-3 sentences, include emotional state and stakes)
> [What was happening? Who was involved? What pressure existed? How did you feel entering this situation?]

**Task** (1-2 sentences, include why it mattered and to whom)
> [What was your specific responsibility? What would happen if you failed? Who was counting on you?]

**Action** (3-5 sentences, include what you considered AND rejected)
> [What did you actually do? What alternatives did you evaluate? Why did you choose this path over others? Who did you involve or consult?]

**Result** (2-3 sentences, include costs and unintended consequences)
> [What happened? What went well? What went poorly? What was the collateral damage? Be honest about mixed outcomes.]

**Learning** (2-3 sentences, must describe a genuine change in behavior)
> [What changed in how you think, decide, or lead? What do you do differently now? How would you handle this situation today versus then?]

---

**Follow-Up Ladder Preparation**

For each level, write the question you expect AND your prepared response.

| Level | Anticipated Question | Your Response |
|-------|---------------------|---------------|
| Surface | [The initial prompt] | [Your story opening] |
| Context | [Deeper situation probe] | [Additional context you held back] |
| Decision | [Why this path?] | [Your reasoning process, alternatives] |
| Tradeoff | [What was the cost?] | [Real costs, not minimized] |
| Meta-Reflection | [What about you?] | [Genuine self-insight] |
| Worldview | [How did this change you?] | [Operationalized principle] |

---

**Adaptation Notes**

How does this story change when asked from different angles?

| Question Framing | What to Emphasize | What to Downplay |
|-----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| "Tell me about a failure" | The thing that went wrong and its consequences | The eventual recovery (if any) |
| "Tell me about a disagreement" | The specific points of contention and your reasoning | The resolution (focus on the conflict itself) |
| "When were you wrong?" | The moment of realization and what you missed | Your original justification |
| "Tell me about ambiguity" | The unknowns and how you operated despite them | The clarity that came later |

---

**Vulnerability Calibration**

| Zone | Content | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Too sanitized | Facts only, no emotional content, no real stakes | "The project was delayed by two weeks" |
| Safe to share | Real consequences, genuine emotion, professional framing | "I lost the trust of my team for about a month. Rebuilding that was harder than the technical problem." |
| Too raw | Deeply personal, unprocessed, could make interviewer uncomfortable | [Do not include unprocessed trauma, ongoing legal matters, or stories you have not yet made peace with] |

**Rule of thumb**: If you cannot tell the story without your voice changing or your chest tightening, it is too raw for an interview. You need more processing time before it becomes a usable story.

## Required Story Categories

Your bank MUST include at least one story for each of these categories. Ideally, some stories cover multiple categories.

### 1. A Project That Failed (Genuinely)

Not "a project that almost failed but I saved it." A real failure with real consequences.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Do you take ownership or distribute blame?
- Can you articulate what went wrong without defensiveness?
- Is the learning specific or generic?
- Have you forgiven yourself while still holding the lesson?

**Strong example opening**: "We lost the contract. $1.2M in expected revenue, gone. And the root cause was a decision I made in month two that I defended for too long."

**Weak example opening**: "There was a project that didn't go as planned, but we learned a lot from it and applied those lessons to the next project."

### 2. A Significant Disagreement with a Manager or Leadership

Not a minor tactical disagreement. A genuine conflict about direction, values, or priorities.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Did you advocate effectively or just comply/rebel?
- Do you understand the other side's reasoning?
- How did you manage the relationship through and after the conflict?
- What would you do differently about the disagreement itself?

**Strong example opening**: "My VP wanted to ship the feature without the privacy safeguards I'd flagged. We had three conversations about it over two weeks, each one more heated than the last."

**Weak example opening**: "There was a small disagreement about technical approach, but we talked it out and aligned quickly."

### 3. A Time You Changed Your Mind on a Deeply Held Technical Opinion

Not changing a preference. Changing a conviction.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Was the original opinion genuinely held (not a straw man)?
- What evidence changed your mind?
- How long did it take? (Instant changes are suspicious)
- Did you update publicly or quietly?

**Strong example opening**: "For five years, I argued that microservices were always the right architecture for teams above 20 engineers. I was wrong, and it took a painful year-long migration to convince me."

### 4. A Situation Involving Ethical Trade-offs

Not a clear-cut ethical violation. A genuine gray area where reasonable people disagree.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Can you hold complexity without collapsing to a simple answer?
- Do you have a framework for ethical reasoning, or do you go with gut?
- Did you consult others or decide alone?
- Are you comfortable with the decision, or does it still bother you?

**Strong example opening**: "We had user data that could improve our model significantly, but the consent language in our ToS was ambiguous. Technically legal, ethically questionable. I had to make the call."

### 5. A Time You Mentored Someone Through Difficulty

Not onboarding a new hire. Helping someone through a genuine professional or personal challenge.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Did you invest real time and emotional energy?
- Did you respect their agency or impose your solution?
- What did YOU learn from mentoring them?
- Is the relationship still healthy?

### 6. Operating in Extreme Ambiguity

Not "the requirements were unclear." A situation where the right answer was genuinely unknowable.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- How do you make decisions with incomplete information?
- Do you freeze, rush, or find a middle path?
- How do you communicate uncertainty to stakeholders?
- Can you distinguish between "I don't know yet" and "this is unknowable"?

### 7. Someone Else Was Right, You Were Wrong

Not a graceful concession. A moment where your ego had to yield to reality.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Can you name the specific person and their specific idea?
- Do you credit them genuinely or grudgingly?
- How quickly did you update once you realized?
- What was the internal experience of being wrong?

**Strong example opening**: "My junior engineer, Priya, told me our caching strategy was going to cause consistency issues under load. I dismissed her concern because she'd only been on the team for three months. She was right. We had a production incident two weeks later."

### 8. Why You Want to Work on AI Safety Specifically

Not "AI is interesting." A specific, personal, genuine connection to the mission.

**What interviewers listen for**:
- Is this a recent interest or a deep one?
- Can you articulate specific aspects of AI safety that concern you?
- Have you done anything concrete to act on this interest?
- Is your concern informed by technical understanding or just headlines?
- Do you acknowledge the difficulty and uncertainty of the problem?

**Strong example opening**: "Three years ago, I was building a content moderation system and realized that my model's false positive rate was disproportionately affecting content in non-English languages. That moment -- where I saw how a well-intentioned system could cause real harm at scale -- changed my career direction."

## Story Bank Health Check

After building your bank, audit it against these criteria:

### Coverage Matrix

Fill in story numbers that cover each category:

| Category | Story #s | Coverage |
|----------|----------|----------|
| Failure & Learning | | Min 2 stories |
| Conflict & Disagreement | | Min 2 stories |
| Mission & Motivation | | Min 1 story |
| Self-Awareness & Growth | | Min 2 stories |
| Ethics & Trade-offs | | Min 1 story |
| Ambiguity & Uncertainty | | Min 1 story |

### Diversity Checks

- [ ] Stories span at least 3 different companies/projects
- [ ] Stories span at least 5 years of career
- [ ] At least 2 stories feature you in a supporting/learning role (not the hero)
- [ ] At least 1 story has no clean resolution
- [ ] At least 1 story involves a peer or junior being right over you
- [ ] No more than 3 stories from the same company/era
- [ ] Stories include both technical and interpersonal dimensions

### Red Flag Audit

Read through all stories in sequence. If any of these are true, revise:

- [ ] Every story ends with a clear positive outcome (add unresolved ones)
- [ ] You are the hero in every story (add ones where others shine)
- [ ] No story mentions a specific person by name (add specific credits)
- [ ] Every failure is ultimately redeemed (add genuine losses)
- [ ] All stories are from the last 2 years (add range)
- [ ] No story makes you uncomfortable to tell (you are over-sanitizing)
